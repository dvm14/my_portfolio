Overview

In this data storytelling project, we explored global music streaming trends and listener insights by diving into the listening activity of music fans around the world. Our central question: Is music streaming really global?

Using a dataset of approximately 5,000 user observations spanning from 2018 to 2024, we performed comprehensive exploratory data analysis and data preprocessing to uncover patterns in how people consume music across different countries, platforms, and genres. Through careful analysis and visualization, we told a compelling data-driven story about the state of music streaming worldwide.

üéµ Global Music Streaming Dashboard

The Dataset

Our dataset represented user listening activity from 2018-2024, with each observation capturing a unique user and their streaming behavior. The dataset was remarkably clean with no missing values and contained both numerical and categorical features that painted a comprehensive picture of listening habits.

Numerical Features

  • Age (13-60 years old)
  • Minutes Streamed Per Day (10-600 min)
  • Number of Songs Liked (1-500 songs)
  • Discovery Weekly Engagement (10-90%)
  • Repeat Song Rate (5-80%)

Categorical Features

  • Country (10 countries)
  • Streaming Platform
  • Top Genre
  • Most Played Artist
  • Subscription Type
  • Listening Time

Methodology & Analysis

Basic Data Analysis

We began with fundamental statistical analysis to understand our data's distribution and characteristics. Our analysis revealed that for all numerical features, the mean and median (50th percentile) were nearly identical, indicating roughly symmetrical distributions with no significant skewness or kurtosis.

Feature Engineering: Artist Country Mapping

One of our key innovations was creating a new feature that mapped each user's "Most Played Artist" to the artist's country of origin. This allowed us to explore whether listeners predominantly consume music from their own country or embrace truly global content.

Key Discoveries

Was It Really Global?

Our analysis led us to some thought-provoking conclusions about the "global" nature of music streaming:

  • Limited Geographic Representation: With only 10 countries represented, the dataset cannot truly claim to represent global listening activity. Major regions like Africa, the Middle East, and much of Asia are absent.
  • Sample Bias: Only listeners from certain backgrounds were included in the sample, potentially skewing results toward specific demographics and listening patterns.
  • Suspicious Results: Some findings raised questions about data representativeness - for example, why would certain artists be overwhelmingly popular in unexpected countries?
  • Western-Centric Data: The majority of featured artists were from English-speaking countries, particularly the US and UK, suggesting the dataset may better represent Western streaming habits than truly global patterns.

What I Learned

  • Critical Data Evaluation: Learned the importance of questioning data sources and being transparent about dataset limitations. Just because data claims to be "global" doesn't mean it truly represents worldwide patterns.
  • Feature Engineering Impact: Creating the artist-country mapping feature was crucial for uncovering deeper insights about music consumption patterns. Sometimes the most valuable features aren't in the original dataset.
  • Data Storytelling: Developed skills in crafting a narrative from data, guiding the audience through our findings in a logical and engaging way rather than just presenting statistics.
  • EDA Best Practices: Gained experience with systematic exploratory data analysis, from understanding distributions and checking for missing values to grouping data by different features to reveal patterns.
  • Visualization Design: Learned to choose appropriate visualizations for different types of insights, whether heatmaps for geographic patterns, distributions for numerical features, or grouped analyses for categorical relationships.
  • Statistical Interpretation: Understood how to interpret distribution statistics (mean, median, skewness) and what they reveal about data characteristics.
  • Collaboration: Working with a partner taught me how to divide analysis tasks, share insights, and combine our findings into a cohesive story.
  • Ethical Data Analysis: Recognized the responsibility to acknowledge dataset limitations and biases rather than presenting findings as absolute truths, especially when dealing with sensitive topics like cultural representation.
